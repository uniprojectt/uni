Risks of sugar

-There is no doubt that sugar has many harmful effects. Sugar is the main cause of hyperglycemia, and it naturally raises insulin in the body. Sugar is beneficial in small amounts, but eating too much of it can lead to weight gain, acne, and type 2 diabetes, and can increase the risk of many serious medical conditions.


-Can cause weight gain

Obesity rates are on the rise around the world, and evidence suggests that added sugar often from sugar-sweetened beverages is a major contributor to obesity. In addition to fast food and unhealthy food that cause obesity, sugars are an essential factor and constitute a large percentage of obesity.


May increase the risk of heart disease

High-sugar diets have been associated with an increased risk of many diseases, including heart disease, which is the number one cause of death worldwide. Evidence suggests that high-sugar diets can lead to obesity and inflammation as well as high levels of triglycerides, blood sugar and blood pressure all of which are risk factors for heart disease. In addition, consuming too much sugar, especially from sugar-sweetened beverages, has been linked to atherosclerosis, a disease characterized by fatty deposits and clogged arteries.


Associated with acne

A diet high in refined carbohydrates, including sugary foods and drinks, has been associated with an increased risk of acne. High GI foods, such as processed sweets, raise blood sugar more quickly than low GI foods. Eating sugary foods can cause blood sugar and insulin levels to rise, leading to increased androgen secretion, oil production, and inflammation — all of which play a role in the development of acne.


Increases the risk of type 2 diabetes

Diabetes is a leading cause of mortality and reduced life expectancy. Its prevalence has doubled over the past 30 years, and projections estimate that its burden will continue to rise. Eating high amounts of sugar can indirectly increase your risk of diabetes by contributing to weight gain and excess body fat – both diabetes risks. Obesity, often caused by excessive sugar consumption, is the strongest risk factor for diabetes

Processed food may contribute to changing your mood and emotions, and may even increase your chances of developing depression. While a healthy diet can help improve your mood, following a diet rich in added sugar.


It drains your energy

Foods high in added sugar cause blood sugar and insulin levels to rise quickly, resulting in increased energy. However, this spike in energy levels is transient. Products loaded with sugar but lacking in protein, fiber, or fat produce a brief energy surge quickly followed by a sharp drop in blood sugar, often referred to as a crash. Constant fluctuations in blood sugar can lead to significant fluctuations in energy levels

How do I reduce the sugars that enter my body on a daily basis?

Lifestyle changes may help avoid type 2 diabetes, the most common type of the disease. It is especially important to follow prevention methods if you are at greater risk of developing type 2 diabetes due to being overweight or obese, having high cholesterol, or have a family history of diabetes. If you've been diagnosed with prediabetes (high blood sugar below the level that indicates diabetes), lifestyle changes may help you avoid or delay diabetes. Making some simple changes to your lifestyle may also help avoid serious health complications of diabetes in the future, such as nerve atrophy, kidney failure, and cardiomyopathy. Start modifying your lifestyle,

Get rid of excess weight

Losing weight reduces the risk of diabetes. In a large study, participants reduced their risk of developing diabetes by about 60% after losing about 7% of their weight by making changes to exercise and diet. The American Diabetes Association recommends that people with prediabetes lose weight by at least 7% to 10% to prevent the disease from progressing. The greater the rate of weight loss, the greater the health benefits. Set a weight loss goal based on your current weight. Consult your doctor about short-term goals and achievable expectations, such as losing 1 to 2 pounds per week

Do more physical activities.

There are many benefits of regular physical activity. Exercise can help you:Weight loss Reducing blood sugar Increase your sensitivity to insulin, which helps keep your blood sugar within the normal range Here are some goals for most adults to promote weight loss and maintain a healthy weight: Aerobic exercises. Aim to spend 30 minutes or more doing moderate to vigorous aerobic exercise — such as brisk walking, swimming, cycling or jogging — most days for a total of at least 150 minutes per week. Resistance exercises. Resistance exercises - at least two to three times a week - increase body strength, balance, and the ability to enjoy an active life. Resistance exercises include weight lifting, yoga, and gymnastics. Reducing inactivity. Breaking from long bouts of inactivity, such as sitting in front of a computer, can help control blood sugar levels. You can spend a few minutes standing, walking around, or doing some light activity every 30 minutes.

Eat healthy plant foods

Plant foods provide the body with vitamins, minerals and carbohydrates. Carbohydrates contain sugars and starch, which are sources of energy for the body, along with fiber. Dietary fiber, also known as roughage or bulk, includes plant food particles that the body cannot digest or absorb. It is noteworthy that foods rich in fiber help in losing weight and reducing the risk of diabetes. Make sure to eat a variety of healthy foods rich in fiber, including: For fruits such as tomatoes, peppers, and fruits that bear fruit on trees Non-starchy vegetables, such as leafy green vegetables, broccoli, and cauliflower Legumes such as beans, peas and lentils Whole grains, such as whole-grain pasta and bread, whole-grain rice, whole-grain oats, and quinoa Benefits of fiber: Slow down the absorption of sugars and reduce blood sugar levels Impede the absorption of fatty foods and cholesterolManaging other risk factors that affect heart health, such as blood pressure and inflammation Reduce the amount of food, as foods rich in fiber are more satiating than others and rich in elements that provide the body with energy Avoid foods that contain “bad carbs,” which are foods high in sugar and low in fiber or nutrients: white bread and pastries, pasta made with white flour, fruit juices, and processed foods that contain sugars or high-fructose corn syrup

Eat healthy fats

Fatty foods contain high levels of calories and should be consumed in moderation. To help lose and control weight, your diet should include: Diet A variety of foods that contain trans fats , which are sometimes called "good fats." Unsaturated fats, whether monounsaturated or polyunsaturated, promote healthy blood cholesterol levels and good cardiovascular health. Good fat sources include: Olive, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed and canola oil Nuts and grains, such as almonds, peanuts, flaxseeds and pumpkin seeds Fatty fish, such as salmon, mackerel, sardines, tuna and cod As for saturated fats, or what are called “bad fats,” they are found in milk and meat derivatives. Your diet should contain a small amount of these fats. You can limit the amount of saturated fat in your diet by eating low-fat dairy products, chicken, and lean red meat.

When to visit your doctor

The American Diabetes Association recommends routine screening with diagnostic tests for type 2 diabetes for all adults age 45 and older, for the following groups:People who are under 45 years of age and who are overweight or obese, as well as one or more diabetes risk factors Women with gestational diabetes People diagnosed with prediabetes Children who are overweight or obese, a family history of type 2 diabetes, or other risk factors Ask your questions about diabetes prevention to your doctor. Your doctor will appreciate your efforts to prevent diabetes and may make additional suggestions based on your medical history or other factors.
